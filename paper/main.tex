\begin{abstract}
Robot learning often treats invariance as a virtue: if lighting, viewpoint, pose, or texture changes, the policy should remain stable. But some transformations are physically invalid. A mirror-handed contact, friction swap, tool-affordance swap, or temporal-order reversal can require a different action even when visual features look statistically related. We rebuild Paper 108 around a falsifiable claim: robot policies should audit whether an invariance is physically valid before enforcing it. The local benchmark covers 5 task families, 7 invariance regimes, 5 stress splits, 9 methods, 7 seeds, and 84 episodes per group. On combined invariance stress, the proposed physical-invariance audit obtains $0.685 \pm 0.008$ success versus $0.612 \pm 0.006$ for the strongest non-oracle baseline, improves invalid-invariance rejection from $0.476$ to $0.619$, reduces false-invariance acceptance from $0.123$ to $0.076$, and reduces contact violation from $0.060$ to $0.044$. Pairwise seed tests favor the proposed method over the strongest baseline in 7/7 seeds, and ablations show that physical-validity classification, contact-frame testing, affordance consistency, temporal-order checking, and conservatism calibration each matter. The terminal decision is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}: the local evidence supports the mechanism, but the paper is not ICLR-main-ready until validated on real robots or independent high-fidelity benchmarks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}
Invariance and equivariance are central tools in modern machine learning. Group equivariant networks explicitly encode transformation structure \citep{cohen2016group}, while domain randomization uses broad nuisance variation to improve transfer \citep{tobin2017domain}. Invariant risk minimization seeks representations that remain stable across environments \citep{arjovsky2019invariant}. In robot manipulation, Transporter Networks and related equivariant methods exploit spatial structure to improve rearrangement and manipulation generalization \citep{zeng2020transporter}.

Robotics adds a complication: transformations can change physics. Lighting and background shifts are often action-preserving. Small object-frame pose changes can be equivariant. But mirror-handed contact, changed friction, swapped tools, support-surface changes, or reversed temporal order can invalidate the original action. This paper asks whether robot policies should audit an invariance for physical validity before enforcing it.

\section{Method sketch}
The proposed method, \texttt{proposed\_physical\_invariance\_audit}, separates action-preserving transformations from action-changing transformations. The audit scores:
\begin{itemize}
\item valid perceptual invariance retention;
\item valid geometric equivariance;
\item invalid physical-invariance rejection;
\item contact-frame consistency;
\item affordance consistency;
\item temporal-order consistency;
\item conservatism and intervention cost.
\end{itemize}

The implementation is an executable diagnostic benchmark rather than a trained neural robot policy. That is enough for a local mechanism audit, but not enough for a final empirical robotics submission.

\section{Benchmark}
The rebuilt benchmark is generated by \texttt{src/run\_experiment.py}. It writes aggregate CSVs, figures, and LaTeX tables directly, preserving coverage while staying lightweight.

\paragraph{Tasks.}
The tasks are peg insertion, drawer opening, cable routing, tool use, and mobile pick-and-place.

\paragraph{Invariance regimes.}
The regimes are lighting/background shift, camera viewpoint shift, SE(2) pose transform, mirror-handed contact, material/friction swap, tool-affordance swap, and temporal-order reversal.

\paragraph{Splits.}
The splits are nominal, valid-perceptual shift, valid-geometric shift, invalid-physical shift, and combined invariance stress.

\paragraph{Methods.}
The baselines are no-invariance behavior cloning, aggressive augmentation, equivariant policy, domain randomization, invariant-risk-minimization proxy, contrastive invariance learner, and conformal shift filter. The benchmark also includes the proposed audit and an oracle invariance-validity supervisor.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
The evidence reports task success, valid-invariance retention, invalid-invariance rejection, task-equivalence F1, false-invariance acceptance, contact violation, unsafe action, calibration ECE, intervention cost, data-efficiency proxy, regret to oracle, paired seed differences, ablations, stress sweeps, and failure cases.

\section{Where the method still fails}
The failure-case CSV shows that the audit helps least when the transformation is genuinely valid or when a conformal filter can cheaply reject it. Those are intended boundaries. The method's value appears when an invariance appears statistically plausible but is physically invalid because contact, material, affordance, or temporal order changes.

\section{Hostile prior-work boundary}
Group equivariant networks, domain randomization, invariant risk minimization, and transporter-style manipulation already make invariance and equivariance central to robot learning \citep{cohen2016group,tobin2017domain,arjovsky2019invariant,zeng2020transporter}. The present paper survives only under a narrower interpretation: it is about \emph{physical validity of invariances}, not invariance learning in general. If real robot experiments later show that augmentation, equivariance, domain randomization, IRM, or conformal filters match the proposed audit on success, invalid-invariance rejection, false acceptance, contact violation, unsafe action, and ablation robustness, the claim should be killed.

\section{Limitations and terminal decision}
The evidence is strong enough to justify continued work but not submission. The limitations are material:
\begin{itemize}
\item the benchmark is local and synthetic;
\item no real robot or independent high-fidelity simulator has been used;
\item baselines are executable diagnostic models, not external trained robot systems;
\item no learned policy checkpoint, training curve, or deployment video is provided;
\item no external benchmark such as LIBERO, RLBench, Meta-World, BridgeData, or a hardware manipulation suite is reported.
\end{itemize}

The terminal decision for this rebuild is therefore \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}. The next honest version must add real robot or independent high-fidelity evidence, implemented learned baselines, and qualitative rollouts. Without that evidence, the paper should not be submitted to ICLR main.
